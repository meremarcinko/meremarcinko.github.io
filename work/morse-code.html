Building an Accessible Morse Code Learning Platform

A year-long senior capstone project to design and build a React-based educational website for Community Vision — a Portland non-profit serving children and adults with physical disabilities.

Overview

For my senior capstone at the University of Portland, my team of five Computer Science students partnered with Community Vision, a Portland-based assistive technology non-profit. Our task was to build a website that teaches Morse Code through games — giving children and adults with physical disabilities a new way to communicate using just two switches or keyboard keys instead of 50+. I served as the team's primary client liaison and lead UI/UX designer. The project also won Best Computer Science Capstone at the University of Portland in 2021. 🏆

The Problem

Making communication accessible — and actually fun.

Children and adults with physical disabilities often rely on assistive technology to communicate, but many learning systems are inaccessible, frustrating, or not engaging enough to hold attention. Community Vision needed a fun, fully customizable, switch-accessible platform that could teach Morse Code progressively — from complete beginner to advanced — without overwhelming users or requiring constant adult assistance.

My Contributions

Client liaison, UI designer, illustrator, and developer.

I was the primary point of contact between our team and Community Vision, scheduling and leading all client meetings throughout the year. On the design side, I was responsible for the home page, Getting Started page, and About page — the three pages every user sees first. I also hand-drew all the cartoon game icons to give the platform a warm, playful personality that would appeal to both kids and adults. On the development side, I contributed to game development in React, HTML, CSS, and JavaScript.

Accessibility

Designed for everyone — customizable by each user

A core principle of the project was that no two users are the same. We built a full setting system allowing users to customize background color, dot and dash button colors, text color, font size, game speed, and volume. I researched color theory and contrast ratios to ensure our default settings were accessible out of the box, while the customization system gave users full control over their own experience.

Real Users, Real Feedback

Tested with people it was built for.

One of the most meaningful parts of this project was watching real Community Vision clients — both children and adults — use physical switches to play the game we built. Their feedback directly shaped our design decisions, from simplifying the background to adjusting game speed defaults. Seeing someone use something you built to communicate is a feeling that's hard to put into words.

What I learned

Accessibility isn't a feature — it's a foundation.

This project taught me what it really means to design for people, not just users. Watching real children and adults use switches to navigate something our team built from scratch was one of the most meaningful experiences of my career so far. I learned that accessibility has to be built in from the start, not added at the end. I also grew as a developer, learning React from scratch while simultaneously managing client relationships and leading the visual design. And I learned that I can draw game icons that make kids smile — which might be my favorite outcome of all.
