Overview

The Multnomah Athletic Club is one of Portland's most storied private athletic clubs. Its website had grown organically over decades — accumulating outdated pages, inconsistent department layouts, and content that was difficult to find and harder to maintain. I joined the project in Phase 2 as Website Operations Coordinator, tasked with helping restructure and rebuild the site from the inside out.

The Problem

A website that hadn't kept up with the club.

The MAC website felt like it hadn't been touched in 20 years. Different departments had built their own pages independently, resulting in a site with no visual consistency, an overwhelming amount of content, and a search function that felt broken. Members struggled to find basic information, and the sheer volume of pages — many outdated or redundant — made the site feel irrelevant and unpleasant to navigate.

The Process

Jumping in mid-project and making an impact.

1

Content Audit

Working in NorthStar CMS, I inventoried and removed outdated content across the entire site — resulting in 96 pages and 150+ images removed.

2

Department Coordination

I participated in 20+ cross-department meetings, observing and documenting feedback from department leads about their content needs and pain points.

3

Structure Planning

Working with the project lead, I helped map out the new information architecture based on department feedback — figuring out what to keep, what to cut, and how to organize it.

4

Mockup Building

Using the design team's style guide, I built page mockups starting from whiteboard wireframes, then producing PDF mockups for the content team to review and mark up.

5

Aquatics Page

I designed a full mockup for the Aquatics department page — starting from a whiteboard session, incorporating department feedback, and producing a polished mockup. Elements of my design were drawn from by the project lead when building the final version.

What I Learned

Website problems are people problems first.

This project taught me that getting alignment across departments — each with their own priorities — requires patience, clear communication, and learning how to work effectively within an established team dynamic. I also grew significantly in my ability to use AI as a practical design tool, and deepened my understanding of how large websites are structured and maintained. One of the most valuable skills I developed was learning how to translate messy, real-world feedback into clean, structured page designs.
