Redesigning the Santa Clarita Art Association Website

A UX bootcamp capstone project to increase community engagement and make it easier for local art lovers to discover and attend events.

Overview

As part of my UC Berkeley UX/UI Design bootcamp, my team of five was tasked with selecting a non-profit whose website needed help. We chose the Santa Clarita Art Association — a California-based organization struggling with low community engagement and a website that made it difficult for people to find and sign up for events. I took a lead role in directing the team throughout the project.

The Problem

A community that wanted to connect — but couldn't figure out how.

The SCAA website had a lot of heart but a confusing user experience. Navigation was split across two rows with too many options, page layouts shifted inconsistently, and event sign-up was buried and unclear. Community members who wanted to attend local art events simply couldn't figure out how to do it — and many gave up before trying.

User Research

Listening before designing.

We interviewed 10 participants aged 27-62, all of whom had attended art events. We synthesized our findings into an affinity diagram revealing what users wanted, needed, were frustrated by, and hoped to gain from the experience.

The Process

From research to high-fidelity prototype.

1

Stakeholder Interview

A team member interviewed SCAA stakeholders directly. Key takeaways: the site felt drab, event sign-up was confusing, and many events had no attendance.

2

User Persona & Storyboard

We created a persona representing our core user — someone new to the area looking to connect with local artists — and mapped their ideal journey from discovery to event attendance.

3

User Flow

We mapped a streamlined flow focused on event sign-up — the core problem we were solving — giving users two clear paths: browse by calendar or go directly to a specific event.

4

Lo-Fi Sketches & Usability Testing

We sketched three key screens — homepage, events page, and event detail — then tested with real users. Feedback revealed issues with ticket quantity, event layout, and banner sizing.

5

Hi-Fi Prototype & A/B Testing

We built polished desktop and mobile prototypes in Figma. We ran two A/B tests — hamburger menu placement and first-click testing — to validate our design decisions with data.

The Result

A cleaner, more welcoming experience for the whole community.

We delivered desktop and mobile hi-fi prototypes that simplified navigation from 9+ items across two rows down to 5 clear categories, made events the focal point of the homepage, and created a clear direct path to event registration.

What I Learned

Good design starts with listening.

This project was my first experience leading a UX team through a full design process from research to prototype. I learned that our best design decisions came directly from what users told us in interviews — not from what we assumed. I also learned how to synthesize messy research data into clear design direction, and how to keep a team aligned and moving forward under a deadline.
